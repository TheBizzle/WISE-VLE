In the previous activity, you learned about how the elements in the two different kinds of processes have very different ways of interacting. In order to understand a process deeply, not only must we describe the pattern and its elements, we have to understand HOW the pattern is produced from the interactions of the elements. This is called the mechanism of the process, and it has to do with the relationship between two levels of description that we call the "micro" level and the "macro" level.

Let's start by explaining what we mean by the word "levels". This word could refer to the actual, physical levels of something -- like the levels of a cake or the floors of a building. Or, the word could refer to levels of experience or sophistication, like the levels of a video game - when you move up to the "next level". Finally, there can be levels of detail, like the "overview" level, or the "nitty gritty level".

You've probably heard the terms "micro" and "macro" before, but we should also say a bit about how we will use those terms. Micro does not always have to mean "microscopic", but does always refers to a low level of detail. Macro, on the other hand, is typically used to describe the most visible level, or pattern level of a process or object.

But how do we decide what is the micro and macro level of a process? For the purposes of this lesson, we will define the micro level as the level of the elements, and the macro level as the level of the pattern. In the next few pages, we'll show you that it is the relationships between the micro and macro levels of a process that really define whether it is a direct or emergent process.
